Scratchlets Workshop #3 at Agastya
Tuesday, January 21, 2010
Sanjli Gidwaney

Insights taken into account and revisions made - iterative learning at its finest. 

Today, I had to sit down and think of all the things that had gone wrong with the last few Scratchlets workshops. I needed to devise new techniques to teach my class, but how? I started thinking of a scenario in my head and chalking it out on a chalk board. I hate the sound of chalk on a chalk board, it drives me crazy….errgghh. 

Scenario: A boy named Salman is sleeping in his bed (starting position). He wakes up and walks over to a table in his room (turn +45 degrees and move 3 steps). He puts on his hat (change costume). He decides to visit his girlfriend Parvati and walks out of his home (turn +45 degrees and move 5 steps). The path to Parvati’s house is windey and twisty. He must walk forward a few paces and make a sharp turn (move 4 steps and turn -90 degrees). He is so excited to see Parvati, that he randomly starts doing a dance (Repeat - move 2 steps, turn +180), and then turns and continues down the path (turn +45 degrees, move 7 steps). When he finally arrives at Parvati’s house, she is sleeping so he rings the doorbell (play sound). Now here come the conditional statements. When Parvati wakes up and opens the door “if” she shakes Salman’s hand, he says “hello,” “else” he waits/knocks harder (play sound)/turns in a circle. 

I used the scenario mentioned above, to demonstrate the use and purpose of Scratchlets. My thinking in doing this was that children learn best when they’re having fun. Rather than simply explaining the functions, I thought a story would help convey the various pieces on the board. It worked! The children immediately caught on, and you could already see the excitement in their eyes. Instead of my bicycle example, I asked the children how they would prepare the traditional south Indian dish of Sambar (Indian soup) and rice. They talked about all the logical steps one would have to follow, including washing the rice and adding water. I then asked them, what if you put too much salt in the Sambar. They responded, “we would think about what we did wrong, and if there was too much salt, we would either add more water to the Sambar or more salt to the rice, so both dishes would be equally salty.” Maybe not how I think about cooking, but it was still very clever. 

During the demonstration process, my friend Paddy was translating for me (he was awesome!). The rest of the teachers were either on their cell phones or chit chatting. When it came time to execute, the children seem to have a better grasp of the game than the teachers. The same teachers who had helped me previously were asking me questions which were explained during the demonstration. It was becoming increasingly difficult to get their full commitment. One teacher however, recognized my frustration and really stepped up to the plate. Another teacher unfortunately, was really overwhelming the children. It was to the point that the children were freezing up instead of moving forward. After pulling her away, I noticed the children started communicating with one another and started problem solving their way through the path. That’s when I realized that the teachers were making the students feel tense, and when the students were just left to be that’s when they really started to flourish. 

The most important lesson I learned today was that it didn’t really matter if the children were programming each and every station correctly; it was the process and journey of learning that was important. When some of the children were left on their own to try and get through the circuit, they often missed the dance station, and completely forgot about the “if” statement. I had to remind myself, it was okay. They still knew and understood the other pieces and that was a huge step in the right direction. 

Rather than only measuring children on the correctness of their output, teachers and mentors should also account for participation and effort. Without those, children are no longer children they are robots; a dangerous trap that adults tend to fall pray too. 

A funny observation…

There was one group made up of 2 boys and 1 girl. The boys were having all the fun programming away, while the girl was the robot doing as she was being told. Now that’s sexism at its finest…some things never change!

Integrated Learning Curriculum Brainstorming at Agastya
Tuesday, January 20, 2010
Sanjli Gidwaney

What is Integrated Learning Curriculum? No idea, but at the time, it sounded like a good title for the brainstorming exercise I conducted with some Agastya teachers. Today we were brainstorming ways to teach a common theme across multiple subject areas using tools that cater to each of the following learning styles: auditory, visual, tactile, and experiential. 

Just imagine a curriculum whereby students could take a walk through an herbal garden with an ecology instructor to learn about plants and flowers (auditory learning). They could then conduct experiments on how those plants work in the human body (experiential/biology), and finally examine the shape, structure and color of the plants and create sculptures which mimic their function or behavior (visual/art). 

I was challenged today to come up with compelling ways to engage the teachers, build a sense of team unity, and ensure an overall fun experience. I started doing some internal brainstorming when a recent memory entered my head. The first time I met Mr. Raghavan of Agastya was at MIT. After our meeting, we headed to IDEO, one of world’s most recognized industrial design firms. It was at IDEO where Mr. Raghavan tasked myself and 1O IDEO employees to develop ways to teach children concepts of biology and time change. We broke out into 3 small groups, discussed wacky and over the top ideas, drew on sticky notes and posted them up on white boards. I decided to utilize a similar approach to brainstorm with the Agastya teachers. 

Today, I had a group of 20 teachers from various subject areas. I began the workshop by playing an ice breaker game called the Human Web. The teachers sat in a semi-circle around me. One teacher would state an interesting fact about him/her self, hold one part of the string and throw the roll to a colleague across the circle. This process continued for about 10 minutes until a giant web had formed between all the teachers, connecting them in a symbolic way, by their individualism and diversity. 

I explained to the teachers what they’d be brainstorming about that day and why brainstorming was an important part of learning. I also presented two posters, one displaying some of the rules of the workshop e.g., lots of discussion, writing/drawing down ideas, and no criticizing, the other listing different types of learning styles. 

I divided the teachers up into 3 groups and gave them each pens, sticky notes and a white board. I first asked the teachers to brainstorm possible themes that could be taught across multiple subject areas. I asked them to NOT over think their ideas or how they could be applied or taught. Some of the groups came up with up to 30 ideas, which they narrowed down to three. They then presented their ideas to the other groups, and each group identified one theme they wanted to work on. The three themes chosen were: cosmetics, cricket (sport), and the nature/behavior of animals. 

Finally, the teachers were tasked to think of three subject areas that were applicable and how they could teach concepts of those subjects using their theme as an example. They had to keep the various different learning styles in mind while designing their tools. Here are some highlights from the brainstorming workshop:

Cricket

  • Biology
    • Experiential learning - Conduct an experiment about how much players sweat during a cricket game based upon the color of their uniform.  Players wear darker color during one day matches and lighter colors during multiple day matches. 
  • Physics
    • Visual and Auditory – hit a cricket ball with a cricket bat and observe the sound. The teacher can then provide an explanation about sound waves.
  • Chemistry
    • Tactile- Announcers for cricket games need microphones to conduct their commentary, and those microphones require batteries. Children can thus hold, touch and feel different types of batteries and learn about their properties. 

Cosmetics

  • Chemistry
    • Experiential and Tactile – Children can experiment with different raw materials commonly found in cosmetics. They can then compare and contrast the use of natural vs. chemical inputs.
  • Art
    • Visual and Tactile- Can combine different colors to create different shades of lipstick and eye shadows.
  • Math
    • Auditory – The teacher can explain concepts of ratios and ingredient amounts as they relate to cosmetics. 

Nature/Behavior of Animals

  • Biology
    • Auditory- The teacher can discuss theories of evolution. 
  • Physics
    • Visual- Children can watch shows such as the Discovery Channel to learn about bird flight. 
  • Art
    • Visual and Tactile- Children and teachers can observe animals at the zoo and collect different materials in the environment to recreate the animals e.g., use tree bark, bottle caps and rocks, to construct a lion

Each group presented their ideas to the class. All other teachers were encouraged to ask questions and build upon ideas. Many ideas were discussed and lesson plans were revised. At the end of the workshop, I had asked one teacher from each group to provide me with a write up of their lesson plan to be shared and distributed among other members of Agastya. 

Although this was a fun lesson in brainstorming, what I hope the teachers got out of the entire process was an understanding about communication; when they communicate and discuss their visions, their ideas, their goals, new solutions to problems emerge which are full of rich and diverse perspectives. The goal of the workshop today was to encourage the teachers to think of new ways of teaching and work together to enhance the experience of children coming to Agastya. I think that’s what they got out of it, but you never know. Hoping is also part of the learning process, I suppose?

Scratchlets Workshop #2 at Agastya
Tuesday, January 19, 2010
Sanjli Gidwaney

Failure:  Noun, the condition or fact of not achieving the desired end or ends e.g., the failure of an experiment

That’s about how I’m feeling right now. Like an English paper with a big fat F stamped on it, in a thick red marker. Do you ever wonder why teachers use the shade Flaring Red to mark papers anyway? I mean, is that not the definition of “rubbing it in”? Okay, so maybe the workshop today wasn’t  a  complete failure, but every time I do a Scratchlets workshop, it becomes harder to recognize if it’s me that just doesn’t get it, or is it the teachers, students, or simply the software programming gods working against me.…what is it?! If only I knew….the art of problem finding is as important as problem solving (lesson learned, now can we please move on?). 

Today I conducted my second Scratchlets workshop. The children were of the 9th standard this time, an almost equal mix of boys and girls. Earlier this afternoon, I did a re-training of the teachers which seemed to help a little in some areas, not so much in others. I started with the normal goals, explanation of the board, the functions, some examples... My friend Paddy translated beautifully, he even made the children laugh. A nice change from their rather serious and military like demeanor e.g., saluting to greet you, standing when you call their name. 

Frustrating! Again the children were divided into smaller groups, to work with one of the many teachers who were helping out that day. I immediately noticed the teachers, instead of helping the students think on their own, would complete the task for them. In defense of the teachers, they wanted so badly for the children to succeed, they would start taking over the learning process. However, failure is a critical part of learning, the entire point of my workshop rests on the shoulders of this very principle. 

The children would often stare blankly as I tested their understanding of the lesson, signaling something was going wrong somewhere. After a few times around the track, they started catching onto the basic idea, but I still wasn’t sure how much was actually resonating with them. I had made some provisions to the workshop today, including the addition of a reference circle diagram to each board detailing concepts such as clockwise, anticlockwise and degrees. I was hoping this new addition would at least somewhat solve the challenge of angled turning. Wrong! The children hardly looked at the diagram, instead requesting multiple explanations on degrees and direction. It was as if they wanted to be told what to do, instead of trying to figure it out on their own. Eventually they started recognizing the value in the diagram and would use it minimally when prompted. Perhaps, the diagram wasn’t the best learning tool – a lesson for next time.

The debugging process was probably the most interesting. The children were able to see their faults when testing their programs, however, some of those faults were caused by poor instruction on part of the teachers. This was a good way for the teachers to learn problem solving as well. 

When we got to the reflection portion of the workshop, I asked the children what they liked, didn’t like, what they learned, and how it would be useful to them. They seemed to have enjoyed programming their friends, making them dance, and turning them into cartoon like characters, controlling their each and every move. One child said that logical thinking was useful in improving thinking in general – I really liked this kid. One child said the lessons learned in this game could be used to start a business or learning how to be good at your job – BINGO! 

I later went outside to regroup with some of the teachers. Paddy expressed some necessary adjustments (listed below) which he felt would enhance the experience of the workshop. I have really taken his advice to heart and plan on incorporating some of his suggestions and my own insights into the next workshop. 

  1. Don’t use the example of riding a bicycle or programming a website, instead, use  examples that are contextually relevant and humorous e.g., how to make daal, how to eat food
  2. Take the entire class through a demonstration of programming a circuit
  3. Encourage the teachers to allow the children to fail quickly and debug on their own
  4. Structure the reflection discussion around problem solving 
  5. Make the end challenge station more climactic and exciting
  6. Only one teacher should be translating during the introduction/reflection discussion
  7. During reflection only ask one child one question – when children know they will be asked multiple questions, they tend to shy away from participating in the group discussion

Microsoft Research and Infosys
Monday, January 18, 2010
Sanjli Gidwaney

Hmmm, where do I begin…how about yesterday, where I spent an awesome day at Microsoft Research and the famous Infosys campus in Bangalore? 

At Microsoft, I met Ed Cutrell, the head of Technologies for Emerging Markets. His team is exploring innovative technologies in areas such as education and agriculture for developing communities. One such example is MultiPoint, a system which allows several children to simultaneously use one computer with more than one mouse. In addition, Ed’s team is finding opportunities to deliver educational content and interactive games using specially designed DVDs which play on a common household DVD player; a device which often can be found in both rural and urban environments regardless of income level. Through an in-depth ethnographic study conducted by Ed’s team, it was found that most households already have the computing power in their DVD player and TV to deliver many of the same experiences offered by more expensive alternatives, including computers and laptops. For more information, click here: http://research.microsoft.com/en-us/groups/tem/default.aspx

My favorite moment of yesterday was riding a bicycle, around what is probably the coolest office grounds I’ve ever seen. I mean, how would you like it if your office was set in a serene oasis surrounded by lush gardens, coy ponds, stunning fountains, open air canteens, tennis courts, swimming pools, and fresh juice stalls etc. With bicycles strategically placed around the campus, employees can freely and easily move around from one building to another, of which there are more than 50. 

While on our way out, we ventured into the conference center where we read a notice for a talk being given by Dr. Charles Vest, former president of MIT. The talk had drawn in heads of NGOs, thought leaders, notable scholars and influential business owners. We decided to stay for Mr. Vest’s lecture, where he discussed the importance and role of creativity and multi disciplinary approaches to education, especially in the realm of engineering. He also spoke about the ideal MIT student; someone who doesn’t necessarily have the highest academic standing, rather is willing to dive off cliffs, takes risks, and achieve new heights. It was at that moment, that I took out my pad of paper, and quickly drew a stick figure jumping off a mountain top. I passed my doodle drawing to my husband, Vinay, and whispered, “that’s me!”

We arrived back at our hotel around 11pm, tired and exhausted. It’s surprising how quickly the novelty of shuttling around in rickety rickshaws, smelling of mixed masala and body odor, wears off. 

We slept for a few hours and got up at around 4am. When we ventured downstairs to the hotel lobby, we were greeted by our very incompetent concierge, an interaction I could’ve done without. He had hired us a rip off cab to drive 5 minutes to the nearest train station, a scam we’ve become accustomed to over our last few visits to India; the concierge works out a deal with the cab driver which involves ripping off passengers and sharing the profits. After much arguing we negotiated the rate down to half, and jetted off to the train station. 

When we finally boarded the train back we noticed there were no seats available. We ended up standing for the 1.5 hour journey, being bumped by chai, dosa, Lays Chips, Biscuit and coffee wallas. Everytime they passed, about every 5 minutes, they would say “side, side” which meant move out of the way. At one point I was so annoyed I said, “I’ll show you side, how about OUTSIDE?!”As if that wasn’t bad enough, the chai walla was serving an old man who was wearing a traditional white lungi (a cloth skirt worn primarily by village men), when it came time to pay, the old man pulled up his lungi to his waist, reached into his boxer shorts, pulled out his wallet and handed the chai walla a 10 Rupee note. Yuck! The chai walla didn’t seem so bad anymore!

 

E-Textiles Workshop #2 at Agastya
Tuesday, January 13, 2010
Sanjli Gidwaney

Today I worked with a group of all girls, sweet, wonderful, and innocent 7th graders excited to learn but timid in nature. They walked into the classroom in an orderly fashion, and quickly sat down to observe the lesson. I made several tweaks to the workshop, which I was looking forward to testing. I smiled at my guinea pigs for the day, “good afternoon everyone” I said, “good afternoon madam, they responded.”

I started with the regular spiel about the goal of the workshop, and what they’d be learning and doing. The girls worked quietly without any conversation – it was hard to break their concentration and encourage them to chat and have fun along the way.

A few minutes later, in walked Ramji, the head of the organization. Everyone stood at attendance, slightly fearful, slightly in awe of his commanding, direct yet endearing nature. It is without question that Ramji’s dedication to Agastya, is at the heart of the organization’s ability to reach millions of children across South India. While I explained my workshop to Ramji, he seemed pleased with the fact that not only were the students learning new concepts and principles, the teachers were as well. We broke for lunch where I continued the conversation with Ramji about possible brainstorming workshops with teachers, the young instructors program, and how to take my workshops forward.

I returned to my class at 2:30pm and encouraged the girls to quickly finish up their designs. A handful of boys also requested to join my class. Then the fun really began. The teachers performed a role playing exercise, whereby a small path was laid on the ground in the form of the letter U. On one end stood Rama (positive figure in Hindu mythology) with Hanuman (monkey god) and friends (monkeys), on the other, stood Sita, and finally in the center stood Ravana (vileness figure in Hindu mythology). The story went something like this; Rama had requested his friend Hanuman and fellow monkeys to save his love Sita, from the evil Ravana. He instructed Hanuman and fellow monkeys to follow the specific U shaped path and wage a war against Ravana. Once the war was won, Hanuman was to send a signal to Rama by lighting a flame. Hanuman would then proceed to save Sita.

Putting this role play in context of a circuit:

  • Rama- represented the positive charge
  • Sita- represented the negative charge
  • Hanuman’s monkey’s -  represented the electrons
  • Hanuman’s triumph over Ravana – represented  the LED lights
  • The path – represented the flow of electrons from the positive to negative charge 

It was a fun dramatization of electric circuitry, one that seemed to resonate well with the students.

I showed the children how to sew electric circuits, and tested their understanding of circuitry along the way. They would often relate back to the story as a reference. When all the lights started shining brightly, I noticed one girl struggling with her circuit. She immediately realized that she had not sewn her thread through one of the holes of the LED lights. She tried a few different tactics before starting again and problem solving her way until she saw the light go off. I watched closely as she employed her own ingenuity and creative abilities to understand what she had done wrong and make the necessary corrections. Failure is often the best teacher of life.

The children later presented their E-Textiles to the class and this time I encouraged all the spectators to question the designs and their peers understanding of circuitry and electrons. At times, the conversation went off track, too focused on the symbolism of the design. One child even wrote my name on her shirt and explained to the class that “Sanjli teaches E-Textile, and this lesson is like a light, we should all become like lights and teach others.” That really struck a chord in my heart.  When another child asked “why did you use green instead of blue” or “why did you draw a star on one side and a sun on the other, when they cannot be seen together, the child responded “well  it’s my shirt and that’s how I like it.”

Scratchlets Workshop #1 at Agastya
Tuesday, January 12, 2010
Sanjli Gidwaney

I’m seriously and utterly pooped. Slightly frustrated, and slightly relieved. Not sure which one I feel more. I’m sitting on a swing, which ironically is a science model constructed to demonstrate the transference of energy from one object to another. To relieve some tension, I’m singing to myself as I watch a rainbow over the horizon and some Agastya teachers play a game of volleyball.

Today I conducted my first Scratchlets workshop. It didn’t quite go as planned, but I’m forcing myself to look at it as a lesson on what not to do next time. I started the day by conducting a teacher training workshop, explaining the principles and concepts behind Scratchlets. I showed them the Scratch software and explained my involvement with the Lifelong Kindergarten research group at MIT. They seemed to understand, but I couldn’t be sure as in India, when you ask someone “do you understand what I’m saying?” you often receive a typical side to side head nod, which neither means yes or no. I decided to take my chances, which in retrospect was not the smartest choice.

I was given a huge open space for my workshop, where I laid down 3 physical tracks with 4 challenge stations. I had a group of 15 children from the 7th standard who I split up into 3 groups. The objective of the workshop was to teach logical thinking and problem solving using magnetic puzzle pieces, mimicking the interface of the Scratch software.

I gave an introduction of myself translated into Telugu by one of the teachers. The children seemed to understand the general concepts, and displayed a great deal of enthusiasm and willingness to learn. However, given the language barrier and some challenges with the teachers, not all the concepts and principles were being fully communicated and understood. There were also some difficulties with advance circle geometry such as angles and concepts of “if and else statements.” I also felt a little challenged by some of the teachers coming up with their own instructions e.g., changing up the stations or teaching workshop principles in sometimes incorrect ways. With that being said, other teachers excelled in developing new ways of testing the children’s understanding and encouraging them to problem solve through the task at hand.

We reconvened in a big circle where I reiterated the concepts of logical thinking, the importance of problem solving and the need to engage in reflection. When the children were asked if they enjoyed or did not enjoy the workshop, one girl said she did NOT like it. I was a little surprised, but still very much appreciative of her honesty, as I too wanted to find ways of improving the experience for the children. She explained that she did not understand why the workshop was important and that if she was learning principles of programming, why wasn’t she being taught on a computer. Before I could respond to her in my MIT fashion with technology jargon, her classmate Meena, who I would grow to love and admire, responded, “computers are expensive and we cannot all afford to have them.  By playing this game we learned about computer programming and that is an important lesson. We need to learn step by step about how computers work, before we can start using them.” Needles to say, I was floored by her young wisdom and simplicity, as she truly understood the value of the workshop. .

At the end of the workshop, the children broke out into traditional songs and were excited to share with me some of their personal talents. Including a boy named Shiv Kumar, the master of beat boxing and mimicry.

As I sit on the swing and sing and reflect on the fact that although the workshop had not gone as planned, and maybe all the children did not fully grasp what I was trying to teach, I am thankful for their honest attempt and perseverance. I have much to revise in this workshop and am “problem solving” myself along the way. We never really stop problem solving in life do we?

E-Textiles Workshop #1 at Agastya
Monday, January 11, 2010
Sanjli Gidwaney

What a relief?! I just completed my very first E-Textiles workshop. This morning, a large group of children from a school in North Karnataka arrived at the Agastya campus excited and ready to learn.

I started the day by training five male instructors how to conduct an E-Textiles workshop. The teachers all nodded in understanding throughout the training process; however, it wasn’t until I led them through a hands on demonstration that they really started having fun with the various art supplies and materials.  It was extremely important to me that the teachers both understood and enjoyed the learning process as they would partially be leading the workshop with my assistance. Finally, at the end of the training, the teachers had designed a fabulous mini E-Textiles t-shirt. When asked to explain the design they proudly said “The U shape means you. The A is the logo of Agastya. The E means electronics. The star represents a spark. Therefore, when you create an Agastya electronic you become a star.”

We broke for lunch and reconvened at 2pm, at which time I met with 10 male students, who would be my students that day. I placed art supplies and the mini E-Textiles t-shirts to be decorated in the center of four tables. I started the workshop with a simple explanation of what they would be creating over the course of the next 2 hours. I paused every so often to allow one of the teachers to translate my dialogue into Telugu, the local language. Each child decorated his t-shirt with intense concentration. It was challenging to loosen them up, so I went around the classroom and started cracking a few jokes encouraging them to talk amongst each other and have fun. We then engaged in a religious role playing exercise as a metaphor to explain the concept of electrons, positive/negative charges, and LED lights. The children seemed to really enjoy the active participation, especially since I had crafted some simple costumes for them to wear.

Once complete, I showed them the same concept via diagrams on a chalk board, relating each image back to the role play exercise they had just witnessed. I then used the E-Textiles T-Shirt the teachers created earlier that day, as an example of how the diagram and role play related to the to the battery and LED lights on their mini t-shirt they had just decorated. I tasked the children to use conductive thread and a needle to complete one full circuit. Mission accomplished, one by one, the tiny LED lights on each child’s shirt began to light up. So did the smiles on their faces. I was happy with the success rates, minus a few glitches which were a blessing in disguise as they allowed the children the opportunity to debug and problem solve, one of the primary goals of the workshop.

I then asked, what do you think would happen if I snipped a piece of your thread, what would happen to your circuit? One child raised his hand high and responded, “the flow of electricity will stop, the electrons will not be able to reach the light and so the light will not work.” This explanation and demonstration of understanding was critical to the workshop. Just as important was the reflection and explanation of the design of the shirt. Each child had the opportunity to stand up and explain his design. While some explanations were less than original, some offered insights into dreams, likes, and character.

My day was complete, when all the children came up to shake my hand and one in his broken English said to me, “madam, you make very happy me with this shirt.”

Agastya Grand Tour

Sanjli Gidwaney

Its early Saturday morning and the birds are chirping, actually never mind it’s a giant lizard. Who would’ve thought lizards could make so much noise? 

We eat a hearty breakfast and get set for the day. Unfortunately, there is a strike going on in the city and nearby villages given some recent political unrest. As a result, most teachers are on strike. However, this will not stop local children from visiting the Agastya campus today. The Agastya bus left this morning to collect children from nearby government schools. 

It’s about 10am, and I watch amusingly as 50 wide eyed children hop off a colorfully painted bus and line up in an orderly fashion to begin a day full of science and magic. The fourth and fifth grade children have come to spend a half day at Agastya to learn about chemistry, math, and general science. 

I sit in on the chemistry class first, where children are learning about hydrogen chloride, oxygen production and chemical reactions. Along the perimeter of the room lie a host of fun hands on activities specifically designed to teach the core principles of chemistry. The children are awarded “claps” for volunteering and participating in class discussion. The teachers captivate, stimulate and engage children through fun role playing activities, experiments, and the use of real life examples to demonstrates how specific chemicals are used in daily life. The teachers also do a remarkable job of teaching children English along the way. 

I later venture into the art lab, and meet with the Agastya art teacher. He explains his method of sensory learning whereby children explore local materials, including rocks, leaves, and clay and create whatever they want. The art lab is filled with mosaics made from stones and bottle caps, tree branches combined with clay to form animals, and a variety of paper mache projects. I then hop on the Agastya bus, and head to the teacher training center.  A two story building, among other things houses a computer and math lab. I meet with the Agastya math teacher who explains to me the various hands on math tools that have been designed to help children learn concepts such as the Pythagorean theorem, geometry, and general math equations. I’m incredibly fascinated by the brightly colored geometry blocks, origami, and activity sheets. I also have the privilege of meeting the computer science teacher who expresses a desire to teach robotics. 

I’m later taken to the Discovery Center, a new construction built upon a slope, overlooking sprawling mountains, lush vegetation, and a giant river. The building is no short of the Gettysburg museum in LA, however it offers even more unique experience; from where I’m standing now, I can see Karnataka, Andre Pradesh and Tamil Nadu, three states making up the majority of South India. The first two floors of the museum are lined with various science models including a bicycle with square wheels, concave mirrors and a whispering dome. A giant bubble machine awaits me on the bottom floor along with a really fun drawing tool (see pictures below).  This place reminds me of Disneyland!

Before the children leave for the day, they shake my hand and say “bye madam,” and speed off to their buses. What a day?! My jet leg is kicking in and I need a nap. I wake up 3 hours later, eat dinner, and we all head out for a 10pm dessert excursion to a nearby village. Ice cream, yummy!

Arrival at Agastya
Friday, January 8, 2010
Sanjli Gidwaney

Boston to Munich, Munich to Mumbai, Mumbai to Banglore, Bangalore to Kuppum, and 30 hours later I arrive at Agastya, the first of four stops on an incredible research trip to try and understand the Indian education system.  Right now, I cannot tell up from down, night from day, nor breakfast from dinner. My schedule is completely out of whack as I force myself to fight off some extreme jet leg.

It’s been a long drive, and we finally pull up to a dirt road leading into a jungle of fauna and flora. I notice brightly colored butterflies prancing from flower to flower with tremendous ease and grace. We drive up to white cottage situated among an oasis of tranquility. A gentleman by the name of Dr. Shibu comes running out to greet us. I have been corresponding with Dr. Shibu for almost two months regarding my stay. I take a few moments to settle into my room which is clean, sparse and modern, something I was not expecting. It feels good to wash my face and change into some fresh clothes.

I meet with Ms. Rao, the program manager of education for Agastya. We enjoy a light meal together and exchange stories about our lives, interests, and ambitions. I tell her how inspired I am by the work Agastya is doing especially in regards to their focus on hands on and experiential learning to teach core subjects such as math, art, chemistry, physics and others.

We all retire for a few hours of rest before meeting again over a cup of coffee. I gain valuable insights from Ms. Rao about students from neighboring villages who often frequent the Agastya campus and interact with the many teachers and instructors. We discuss some of the challenges they experience including being forced to drop out of school to earn money for their families, malnutrition, and early childhood marriage. I’ve highlighted below some particularly interesting insights Ms. Rao had to offer.


Children

  • Children who frequent the Agastya campus currently attend government schools, whereby the primary model of teaching is visual and single sensory e.g., children learn about the five human senses by reading from a textbook rather than actually using their many senses
  • Children have an innate desire to learn, especially when learning is based on their own personal interests;for them learning is a matter of survival
  • Despite there being a holiday and a regional strike in the State of Andhra Pradesh during, children still want to attend school and continue their education by visiting the Agastya campus
  • Students often tighten their arms and secure themselves in a self embrace when tasked with an unfamiliar challenge– almost a self defense mechanism
  • Children have approximately 30-40 visits to Agastya, whereby they engage in supplementary education through experiments and various learning tools they may not otherwise interact with


Teachers

  • Government teachers sometimes become complacent and lazy with regards to adopting new teaching methodologies and curriculum given job security is not tied to performance
  • Teachers often teach using incorrect pronunciations of words such as jinc instead of zinc or snakes instead of snacks. As a result, children have a rather poor command of the English language and as such experience a great amount of difficulty competing for positions at top universities and job opportunities
  • Teachers need to utilize a more iterative teaching methodology, whereby they start with sensory hands on learning, and graduate to explanation, reading and reflection


Parents

  • Children are often forced to drop out of school at an early age, to conduct house work and increase their parents financial security
  • Parents work vocational jobs in the city and have little time to spend with their children
  • Children rarely understand the financial burden parents face while putting them through school
  • Children have little spare time for homework and play, as house work takes precedence over school work
  • Parents, who strongly believe their children should attend school, often act as catalyst for other members of their community to follow suit
  • The opposite is also true; parents who do not support the education of their children often spread virally negative messages about the importance of schooling
  • Parents need to be educated to help their children’s realize their own potential

 

All children are beautiful and innately possess a spark of curiosity, courage, and creativity. A mentor, teacher, and a friend can act as catalyst to encourage such behavior. Here, at Agastya, children learn to take chances and learn that no answer is a bad answer. Each child is celebrated for their unique abilities, and taught that there is more than one right answer to a problem. Each child has a spark; it’s a matter of sharing that same spark with the rest of the world.  

 

Scratchlets Workshop at the MIT Museum
Sunday, December 13, 2009
Sanjli Gidwaney

I’m sitting on the couch, happy to complete my workshop at the MIT Museum. Today was a test of Scratchlets, a tangible version of Scratch software; a learning tool designed by the Lifelong Kindergarten group at MIT’s Media Lab. I created Scratchlets as a precursor to Scratch, to teach children with limited access to computers the principles and function of Scratch software. 

I struggled last night thinking about the best possible way to introduce my Scratchlets tool to children from a variety of ages and backgrounds. After much deliberation, I thought a game might be the most appropriate route. I started by brainstorming mini tasks children could easily perform within a 20 minute time frame. My biggest challenge was to find ways to introduce children to concepts of cause and effect, engage them in a debugging/problem solving, and encourage them reflect on not only the game, but what they learned while going through the process. So, I designed a game in which children would be tasked to walk from one challenge station to another, using magnetic Scratchlets pieces to program their journey and actions along the way.

This morning I arrived early at the MIT Museum and the wonderful staff helped me set up a table where I would be stationed for the next several hours. I started chalking out a path on the ground using painters tape to represent steps from one station to another. I then marked the numbers 1,2,3,4 representing each station. Ironically, there was another workshop taking place at the same time– a Lego Mindstorms workshop. I was scared that the children would be far more intrigued by the cool robotics than to attend my workshop. 

Alas, I got my first customer, a young boy around 10 years in age, with a limited grasp of the English language. I also had the opportunity to work with a young girl around the same age, who was hesitant and unenthused at first, but started enjoying herself as she jumped from one challenges station to another. I was then approached by a very cheeky skater boy who wanted to use the Scratchlets pieces to program various skate boarding moves. Finally, a mother and son walked to my table curious to find out what Scratchlets was all about. He was my last customer of the day, but boy was he going to make me work. I made the mistake of allowing this child to use me as his character and lead me through the circuit instead of doing it himself. Despite me looking like a fool, I gained some very valuable insights while turning around in circles about a dozen times. 

Some of the more interesting insights I gained were as follows:

Insights:
  1. It is very difficult not to lead children through the exercise e.g., do it for them
  2. Kids read better in upper and lower case, instead of just upper case
  3. Children need to be able to customize tasks e.g., do a dance to something they like such as skateboarding
  4. It is important to communicate the goal of the workshop to parents and mentors for their sign off, and to garner interest
  5. It is very difficult to translate workshop instructions - e.g., for a young boy who only spoke Chinese
  6. Not all children are familiar with concepts of degrees, need important tricks to help them learn quickly
  7. Puzzle pieces are not easy to pick up, but the concept of using physical puzzle pieces works well
  8. Parents and mentors are really interested in the software and are excited to go home and download it
  9. Parents and mentors like hand outs and take aways e.g., Scratch pamphlet
  10. Students have more fun if they’re programming for someone else e.g., instructor/teacher
  11. Children like to build upon a story, and see the result of their actions e.g., full program

Notable Quotes:
  • "This tool is neat because it shows you that some things you can do on the computer you can do in real life. It would be cool though to have a robot follow the circuit/path and do what I did."
  • "I like the online community but can you control your privacy settings? Is it like Facebook?"
  • (After a child plays game and is shown Scratch software) “oh I remember how to do this, I have to put the move button here.”
